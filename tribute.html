Dr. Fritz Haber

A portrait of Dr. Haber

Fritz Haber was one of the most famous chemists from the 1900s. He was born in Breslau, Germany in December 9, 1868 into a middle class Jewish family. He studied chemistry and during his Ph.D. studies he worked on one of the most important industrial chemical reactions still used today. This is of course known as the Bosch-Haber process which is the process by which molecular Nitrogen is converted into ammonia. The Haber process is the process by which abundant chemicals in the air (Nitrogen and Hydrogen) are catalyzed by an iron atom to produce ammonia. Haber studied the production of ammonia from air chemicals using sophisticated and highly experimental equipment. His work led him to a leadership role in the biggest chemical manufacturer in the world, BASF. Haber sold his patent to BASF and became very wealthy.

By 1911, Haber wanted to appeal to German Nationalism so he joined the military. When world war 1 started, Haber founded the chemical weapons divison of the German military and produced chlorine gas bombs. His deadly chemical weapon had concerning lethal effects and became banned after the war in 1919. After the war, Haber recieved the Nobel Prize in chemistry for his work on the Haber process in 1919. Haber remained in Germany as a professor of the Berlin Polytecnic university as head of the department of chemistry until 1933 when Hitler and Nazis drove German jewish scientists to emmigrate from Germany. Haber moved to London in 1931 and died shortly afterward in 1934.
